NewsFeel


Learn about news consumption and engage in discussion




Executive Summary



NewsFeel was created as a mobile app to help people learn about their news consumption and encourage discussion through anonymous, small group conversations.

The inspiration for NewsFeel came from research into filter bubbles. Filter bubbles arise from algorithms that generate similar content and filter out diverse perspectives on social media platforms. They can proliferate the spread of fake news and discrepancies in information.

My role in creating NewsFeel was leading user research efforts from discovery through design phases. Through research, I created studies and facilitated user research sessions to validate ideas which resulted in key features. From research, I led decisions to make our app appealing to people who are motivated to think about shifting their filter bubble, and are interested in conversation to broaden their beliefs around news topics.

Through NewsFeel, the hope is that users feel as though they are not only expanding their horizons in terms of what news they are consuming, but are also learning from others who may have different experiences.

Understanding the Problem Space




Project Goals

As content on social platforms becomes increasingly personalized, individuals’ exposure to diverse media fades due to algorithmic curation. Curation creates filter bubbles, which, according to Merriam Webster, are defined as “online environments in which people are exposed to opinions and information that conform to their existing beliefs.” Our team created three goals to ground our project specifically, to help people: 1. reduce cognitive load in finding new perspectives; 2. empathize and reflect on experiences about news topics; and 3. share and discuss opinions by conversing with diverse individuals in real-time. These goals were based on how we might give people agency over news content to enable them to “burst” filter bubbles, and to broaden their perspectives through interaction. Defining project goals was the first step in understanding the problem space.

Competitor Analysis

Our team analyzed 14 products related to improving news consumption habits. The products fell into four groups regarding their ability to: Report User Habits, Communicate Media Bias, Expose Perspectives, and Audit News Accuracy (e.g. determining political accuracy of news sources). The outcome of the analysis was to realize that no one tool allows users to understand the media they engage with, filter media for fake news, and curate diverse media that may challenge their predisposed beliefs.


Informational Interviews

To understand our potential user groups, I conducted semi-structured interviews to understand people’s habits of reading news and their awareness of filter bubbles. Each interview session lasted about 30 minutes. Our group collected demographic information such as age, occupation and location. As a result of these interviews, we discovered people vary in terms of caring about filter bubbles. Moving into ideation, our team decided to focus on designing for people who care about altering their filter bubble and learning different perspectives. We set our goal to help people who care about diverse perspectives develop more empathy toward others, as they discover news outside of their bubble and engage in discourse.

Ideation and Experiment Design



Storyboarding Ideas

In synthesizing what we learned from our problem space research, we sketched and categorized our ideas. Our ideas were diverse, and ranged in feasibility and creativity. Moderate ideas focused on curation and exposing individual bias. Some creative ideas included gamifing incentives to read news and discovering news on an interactive globe. More extreme ideas included unlocking more diverse content based on reading activity, and using a digital pet with the motivation of keeping it alive by reading diverse sources. User Feedback

To test our ideas from storyboarding, our group did a dozen speed dating sessions with people who could be potential users. We identified these people based on their interest in exposing themselves to opportunities engaging in discussion and diversifying their social media content. From our interviews, we were able to identify favorite storyboards, and get feedback on expressed needs and concerns.

Experiment Design

We hypothesized that individuals interested in similar news topics could converse with each other in anonymous chat rooms and build mutual empathy. To simulate an anonymous chatroom, we used Chatzy which is a free private chat service where users can create a chatroom and invite fellow chat participants via email. Within Chatzy chatrooms, users are only identified by their self-selected username, effectively making them anonymous.

We recruited participants who were interested in social media and news consumption, and were from outside of our class. We conducted two study sessions, with 5 participants in each.


Prior to the study, our team emailed participants a news article along with a high-level overview of the study’s purpose and agenda: participants would read an article and then enter an anonymous chat where they could talk to other participants for up to 30 minutes.


To begin the study, the chatbot (a teammate in the chat) began the conversation by releasing participants' emailed responses to our prompt "Are you interested in this topic? Why or why not?". After that, the conversation was allowed to develop organically. The chatbot teammate would send out pre-determined conversational prompts if needed and, after 30 minutes, notify participants that the chatroom would close.

Following the study, participants received questions about their experience. Of 10 participants, 8 responded. After collecting data from both the observational notes and follow up questions, our team used an empathy map as a tool to trace users’ experiences and to better understand their needs.

Design Prototyping




Evolving Fidelity

For our design, we began by focusing on the two main elements of our app: the chat feature and the process of discovering news. For brevity, I'll speak to iterating on design for the chat which I worked on while teammates focused on news discovery visuals. As part of prototyping, I went through several iterations to improve upon ideas. An initial idea was to have a round table style interaction. Through validation, I found this format placed a high cognitive load on participants to track the flow of the conversation and to anticipate when others would start speaking. This led to separating interaction into the "Room" roundtable and "Chat" discussion components. Through our Chatzy experiment, I adapted feedback to discard the "Room" idea, and incorporated elements of it into the "Chat" interaction which was of higher value for participants.



Feature Alignment

f

As a team, we decided to name our app NewsFeel, since the goal of our app was more than just a novel way of consuming News, but also to connect with others and get a Feel for their perspectives on shared topics. We wanted the overall theme and feeling of the app to be positive and welcoming, so that users felt comfortable and curious to communicate constructively with others about potentially sensitive subject matters. A challenge within our team was balancing the need to appeal to extrovert and introvert user needs. We had considered having introverts observe others chat, but decided it could discourage authentic responses from chat participants. Instead, through the news discovery portion of the app, our team incorporated interaction to engage users at an individual vs. group level to help them learn about their own tendencies and to shape their goals.

Next Steps

Should our team continue to develop NewsFeel, we would experiment expanding new chatroom interactions and features to better group engagement and empathy-building.

Moving from design to development of NewsFeel, from a technical side, we will determine what APIs to use to curate news content. We would also consider how automated moderation (e.g. machine learning) could add a layer of moderation to make the chatroom feel safer for open discussion as NewsFeel gains more users.

Closing Thoughts

Through designing NewsFeel, we created interactions to help users expand their horizons in terms of what news they are consuming, learn from listening to others, and reflect on personal experiences through discourse.

By designing NewsFeel, our goal is for users to feel personally impacted through their experience, empathize with others, and to set an example for broadening perspectives for others close to them.
