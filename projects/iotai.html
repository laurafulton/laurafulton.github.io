IoT AI to Help Kids with Autism


How might we use technology to empower, aid, and improve human connection?





Executive Summary



The //Oneweek Hackathon is Microsoft’s global hackathon dedicated to bringing employees together to collaborate on projects that promote Microsoft’s Mission "to empower every person and every organization on the planet to achieve more.” This project combined Artificial Intelligence (AI) with Internet of Things (IoT) technology to help children with autism recognize facial emotions.

Our project was inspired by a team member's son who has autism and struggles to connect and hold conversations with others. Many children with autism find reading emotions difficult, and it limits their social skills and ability to make friendships.

This project explored the use of real-time image recognition and processing with AI from Microsoft’s Cognitive Services Emotion API. With recognition results, we then provided auditory prompts for conversation based on a recognized emotional context.

Our team conducted research and feedback sessions with children and their parents as we developed ideas, created prototypes, and iterated with code. We also consulted with therapists and, as the project developed created a system for home and therapy use.

Pre-Hackathon Research




User Interviews

In addition to our research and feedback sessions with children and their parents, we also consulted with therapists. As the project progressed, we shifted our focus to developing a learning tool for children to use at home and in therapy sessions. We learned that recognizing facial expressions can be difficult to separate from vocal intonation and body language. While children struggle with emotion recognition, they often have an enhanced ability for ‘systemizing,’ which is the desire to analyze, build, and predict system behaviors.

From conducting user-interviews with a small group, our team made discoveries about the desire for portability, and that the technology should not impede social interaction.

Insights

1. Any image capture should be simple and not impede social engagement.
2. Recognition in real-time can assist children in conversation.
3. Feedback about recognition should be delivered in a sensitive way.



Applying Insights to Design

The findings from background research led to proposing a system with a pocket-sized device running Windows IoT Core as the computer/processor. For image capture, a spyglass camera was proposed. With the Spy Camera, there is no need to have to take out a phone or camera while in conversation. The spy camera also encourages the child to keep eye contact while an image is being captured for recognition. Recognition would take place with Microsoft Emotion Recognition API in the Cloud.

Design Considerations




When will images be captured?

To prevent continuous image capture and recognition, the child can decide when they feel they want emotion feedback. When they decide this, they can press a button to capture a photo of who they are speaking with, and that image is then sent to the cloud for analysis.


How is feedback delivered?

With the emotion API, multiple emotions are recognized and ranked based on percent certainty. For this project, we can tell the child the top emotion recognized and deliver them a prompt based on that emotion.

If a child is engaged in conversation with someone and captures a photo, and the AI decides that the primary emotion is “happy” the audio prompt may be: “Happiness” - ask “How is your day going so far?”

Similarly, if “Sad” is recognized, the audio prompt may be: “Sadness" - ask "Is everything ok? " or if "Angry" is recognized, the prompt may be: “Angry" - ask "Are you upset?"

Coding and Challenges



Focusing on building a training tool

Real-time recognition proved challenging. Additionally, real-world applications for children to wear Spy Glasses in public settings could infringe on the privacy of others since all images captured may not be consensual.

Our team decided to pivot our idea to focus on children using the emotion recognition system as a training tool at home and in therapy sessions.


Pivoting our idea to be used for training sessions: I worked with a front-end designer to transition how audio prompts could enhance displayed information. Our app “IoT Emotion Analyzer” allows children and therapists to view captured images together. The app lends to discussion and can be used as a guessing game on emotion.

Outcome and Next Steps



Pitching at the //Oneweek Science Fair

We were proud to share our work as part of the hackathon final presentations. Our team members who are fulltime Microsoft employees have worked to further develop and implement our work.

Impact and Further Work

At the end of the hackathon, we had children come and try our system. We observed that the children enjoyed turning emotion recognition into a game, visualizing results through the UI.

Our team has received encouraging feedback that suggests children have demonstrated increased emotional awareness and confidence to engage in conversation. Some of the children and others that have followed them have been able to use our application as a learning tool at home and in therapy sessions.
