Improving local fostering through responsive web design



Executive Summary



With this project, we were given a constraint to use responsive web design to improve 'Pet Adoption,' a broad problem space.

"Constraints give us a starting point and some building blocks to work with - a problem to solve, an innovative twist to be revealed, or a person to please." - David Sturt, author of New York Times bestseller Great Work: How to Make a Difference People Love

To narrow the scope and further constrain our focus, we conducted Guerrilla Research, a low-cost form of user research which involves unstructured exploration in the field. Through this, we discovered Biggies Bullies, a local Pittsburgh-based foster organization with a mission of saving and relocating pitbulls.

Constraining our research to pitbull fostering, we were able to focus on a local organization while helping to destigamatize the breed. As we honed in on pitbull fostering, we applied design research: identifying pain points and opportunities through Customer Journey Mapping; highlighting Biggies Bullies service provider and user needs through developing Personas; generating and iterating on ideas through Storyboarding; and receiving feedback on ideas through testing our ideas with Speed Dating.

Our research inspired us to redesign the fostering application as a tangible way to have impact. Thoroughness is important for the foster application, but we asked, why must thoroughness be ambiguous, long, boring, and monotonous? We reimagined the application flow by assessing the information presented in over 50+ textbox questions. We distilled information and designed for clarity. Through Rapid Prototyping, we focused our design ideas to make form filling simple, intuitive, and motivational. Our redesign demonstrated our co-creation of value, adding flexibility to the application for users and providing a higher portion of completed applications for our service provider, Biggies Bullies.

Understanding the Problem Space




Generative Research

We began this project with the topic of "Pet Adoption." To learn more, we conducted research to understand the context, actors, and activities which support and offer challenges in the problem space.

From looking at why pet adoption is needed and what are top concerns faced with adoption, we learned that while shelters prioritize adoption which helps animals in the short-term, shelters also must focus on education so that adopted animals find successful placement and are not returned. Shelters also focus on care and training for animals who come from unfortunate situations e.g. dogfighting, hoarding, or puppy mills.

Through our generative research, we discovered various roles and relationships to be considered: pet adopters, potential adopters, people who run shelters, and people who surrender pets.

We also recognized that "Pet Adoption" is a broad topic: various non-profits focus on adoption for specific types of animals; involvement with adoption can also take form through fostering; and medical and community volunteers provide support through donating time.

Guerilla Research

To understand the pet adoption domain, we visited the Humane Animal Rescue in Pittsburgh to do guerilla research, a cost-effective, quick way to conduct interviews in the field.

While at the Rescue, we met Allison, the owner of Biggies’ Bullies, a Pittsburgh-based Pitbull rescue.

From our interview with Allison, we had two key takeaways:
1. Fostering is a great alternative to adoption because of its lower cost and commitment
2. Pitbulls are widely stigmatized

Honing in on Pitbull Fostering

Reimagining the Fostering Application




Testing Ideas

Considering how to improve the foster process, we explored ideas, ranging from hosting pet parties and providing education seminars to autofill features for more efficient applications. We introduced our ideas into a first round of speed-dating, a process to rapidly sketch and get feedback.

After our first-round of speed-dating, we created final storyboards and contrasted two opportunities: the fostering application or the de-stigmatization of pitbulls. The outcome of speed-dating led us to focus on the fostering application.

Wrangling Information

Thoroughness in the foster application is important. But why must thoroughness be ambiguous, long, boring, and monotonous?

We reimagined the application flow by reassessing the information architecture of the 50+ questions. We asked ourselves what themes were present and how the questions might be grouped. By noticing repeating questions, unclear questions, and where value could by added with the addition of new questions, we brought structure to the application.

It is important to note that information architecture alone was not “responsible” for creating clarity. It was our background research and understanding of the problem space that led us to create 8 categories for a re-designed application.

Rapid Prototyping




Wireframe Designs to Test our Concepts

We created low-fidelity prototypes as an opportunity to test different features for our form. We intentionally sought feedback from peers on different ideas. Some ideas that required iteration were the progress bar at the bottom (was a bit distracting), the section summary at the top of each screen (information could be put somewhere else), and the clock icon (gave the impression to people that they had a time limit).

Realizing Designs through High Fidelity Prototyping

Our goal was to create a form that was not only simple and clear, but also colorful and playful. This would make the fostering application much less daunting to fill out.

To do this, we focused on creating Onboarding Screens, Intermediate Screens, and deliberately made choices for portions of the 8 Application Sections such that information and visuals were tastefully considered.



Prototyping: Onboarding Screens

Giving potential fosters an overview of what to expect is important as it helps Biggies Bullies show that the application is the first step in the process. Following the application, Biggies Bullies visits and assesses the applicant's home. Successful applicants become fosters following an orientation.

Prototyping: Intermediate Screens


We created intermediary screens before each section of the application to serve the following purposes:

1. To inform: The estimated amount of time helps fosterers understand how much time each section will take to fill out

2. To Motivate: Having playful pictures and affirmative quotes motivates the fosterer to continue on with the application

3. To Destigmatize: Some quotes on the page give information that correct misconceptions about pitbulls

Prototyping: Application Sections

In our redesign of the application we made several improvements.

One such improvement: the original application asked “does your municipality allow dogs?”. This may have required a bothersome search by the user. In our redesign, we are able to know municipality guidelines by zipcode.

Another example of rephraming questions comes through displaying images as a way for users to easily answer questions. For example, it is much simpler to select an image of a yard than descrbe a yard through a text answer.

We further distilled questions to make questions easier by focusing on the first time fosterer, who might not understand some terms. In the experience section, definitions are included for terms like neutered and spayed. By clicking on the words, we provide a definition.

Subtle additions, such as adding a word count when text answers were needed, help guide users as to how much information they should provide.
