Family-iar Recipe Assistant


Enhanced cooking brought to you by memories, voice, and wearables





Executive Summary



Giving voice to family recipes is a way to preserve culture through food and relive memories in the kitchen. With a voice cookbook, the Family-iar Recipe Assistant captures family recipes and their associated memories and visual details beyond just recorded instructions.

Through this project, I was part of a small team who collaborated to envision and design scenarios for our assistant. I led the vision for expanding how our voice skill can integrate with visuals. For this, I chose to pursue how voice could be complemented with a wearable device. By prototyping screen for a watch interface, I extended potential for our skill to hand-off information when a screen is available.

This project provided a unique opportunity to touch on how the future of voice assistants involves sharing more than just the weather or another joke.

Why Voice?

Design Brief: model for conversation that contains Remember, Tell Me, and Show Me subtasks. Consider the context and users you are designing for and bring in the very human elements of time and memory, given the limitations of text, limitation of text, utterances, and spreadsheets for capturing enough for a voice user interface (VUI) to access and use in repeated interactions with the same user.

For the scope of the project, I focused on recipes for special occasions (e.g. sweets and dishes for holidays and celebrations). From personal experience, when older relatives write down their cooking process, written format makes these recipes vulnerable. Our team wanted to provide an easy way to digititally capture family recipes through voice-first design.

I defined two goals for the project: 1. To transition familiy recipes from physical to auditory format; and 2. Capture memories associated with recipes to preserve stories for generations to come.

Remember, Conversation

When talking to a voice assistant, to retain context or information from one session to another, storage is needed. A session is defined as a set of interactions that happen in a short period of time. With a typical session, someone could have a conversation asking a voice assistant several things. When they come back a few minutes later, the voice assistant will not remember what was said. Today's assistants have the ability to start learning preferences and associate information with individuals' on a device or account basis.

For storing information associated with recipes such as ingredients, instructions, and memories I used Google Sheets as a database. To do this, I integrated Sheets when I built the Remember It skill. For this assistant, the info the Remember It skill stores is only accessible by devices with permission to access my account. This helps maintain user privacy. For a family who shares recipes, with shared permission they can collaborate on a common cookbook through the Remember It assistant.

Tell Me, Conversation

As well as store information to remember, the Familiar Recipe skill can suggest and guide as it retrieves recipes and memories associated with them.

Listen to the assistant (video at left) help plan for a special event by telling the user about a Mango Sticky Rice recipe that a relative's enjoys and playing their memory associated with it.

Tell Me, Visuals

As part of speaking to the user through voice, the assistant can supplement what is told by simple visuals. With Google Assistant, I was able to extend voice-first to support screen and mobile device experiences. Screened devices help take mental load off of voice-first experiences because the user is not responsible for actively listening to what is said word for word.

×

Show Me, Visuals

In addition to thinking about how the assistant audio can be supplemented with screened devices, I considered how the recipe experience could be handed off. As part of a handoff, I decided that steps for recipes could be enhanced with visuals.

I designed wearable screens for a Mango Pudding recipe to demonstrate how seeing pictures of the recipe's progress while performing steps can validate what the user should be doing while in the cooking process.

Impact and Next Steps


The Familiar Recipe assistant provides a medium to transition recipes from a writen format to an interactive experience.

Voice is an interface which does not require physical interaction. For users who have difficulty reading, auditory information can remove the need to follow text-heavy instructions. Voice also aids users who are vision impaired who find it easier to hear information rather than use a magnifier or screen reader. As speech detection improves, recognition will be more inclusive for those with accents and speech impediments. And as wearable tech adoption grows, gestures will also supplemental interaction.

Next steps for the Familiar Recipe assistant include user testing for voice only interaction as well as scoping how handoffs will be implemented. As part of testing, considering context will also be important to understand how noises and distractions in the kitchen come into play when someone chooses to store a recipe instructions or get advice for what to cook.
